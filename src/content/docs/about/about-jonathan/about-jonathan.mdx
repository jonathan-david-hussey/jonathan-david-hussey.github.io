![Portrait of Jonathan Hussey](./portrait.jpg)

I am a staff-level software engineer with a strong architecture focus and 14+ years of experience designing, rebuilding, and operating systems that have to keep working. The work has ranged from healthcare platforms where concurrent edits to patient data are a safety concern, to analytics pipelines ingesting hundreds of millions of records a month, to AI-enabled systems where the real engineering challenge begins after the model call returns.

My background began in web development and design, then moved through energy, healthcare, social intelligence, government, and enterprise environments. That path shaped the way I approach architecture: identify the assets that matter, make failure visible, preserve enough evidence to recover, and measure the result rather than relying on assertion.

These days I often take architecture ownership while remaining a full hands-on contributor. That usually means defining the system boundary, writing the load-bearing parts, reviewing the rest, and helping the team understand why the design exists. The objective is not only to ship the software, but to leave behind a system that can be operated, explained, extended, and repaired.

## How I Work

- **Design around failure paths.** I treat retries, audit trails, durable queues, explicit state, replayability, and operational alerts as part of the core design rather than as late additions.
- **Measure before and after.** Most of the numbers on this site exist because performance claims without baselines are just adjectives. A change should be able to show what improved, what became safer, or what became easier to operate.
- **Use patterns where they clarify responsibility.** Strategy, Observer, State, Adapter, Builder, Template Method, and Chain of Responsibility appear across the projects and case studies because they create useful boundaries around behaviour, events, construction, and variation.
- **Keep architecture connected to delivery.** A design only matters if it survives the implementation. I prefer small, testable stages, clear contracts, and code paths that can be inspected when something goes wrong.
- **Use AI tools deliberately.** Claude Code, OpenAI Codex, GitHub Copilot, and similar tools are useful accelerators for scaffolding, tests, refactoring, and exploration, but the design, review, and accountability still sit with the engineer.
