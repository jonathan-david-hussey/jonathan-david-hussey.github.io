## Context

The platform is a social-media intelligence service whose search, reporting, and influencer profiles all depend upon a steady supply of Twitter data. The ingestion system therefore had to keep hundreds of thousands of accounts fresh, retrieve new posts without wasting provider calls, recover important gaps in history, and hand everything downstream in a shape the rest of the platform already understood.

The following assets have been identified as central to that activity: the freshness of each account's posts, the provider's finite rate-limit budget, the schedule that decides which account is due, the download requests in flight, the retrieved tweets, the internal document shape consumed downstream, the ingestion-status records, and the last-processed position that allows each refresh to resume cleanly. We are able to regard these as assets because they are essential to the search, reporting, and profile products that rest upon them; any negative impact upon them would affect not a single account but the continuity and accuracy of the entire platform.

This framing turns ingestion into a question of which assets must be protected, and by which safeguards, rather than a single task of calling Twitter and storing the result.

## The Problem

The threats to these assets do not appear at the moment of the first download; they appear in keeping the whole loop healthy under a finite rate budget. A call spent upon an account with no new data is a call denied to one that has some. An unbounded download may page indefinitely or, worse, stop early and without warning. Backfill, which is usually more urgent than routine refresh, may crowd refresh out entirely if the two share a budget without discipline. And the status loop that depends upon the search surface carries a ceiling of its own, independent of the provider's.

The most serious threat was silent under-collection. A long-standing defect in the pagination path capped the yield of each request at a fraction of what was available, quietly starving search, reporting, and profiles of data while presenting no visible failure at all.

The level of risk followed, as it usually does, from the likelihood of a threat combined with the severity of its impact. A silent and recurring loss of data carried a far higher risk than a visible and occasional failure, because the former degrades every dependent product at once and leaves no trace to follow. Our analysis therefore treated pagination, rate discipline, and the separation of concerns as first-class design rather than plumbing.

## Constraints

- **A finite provider rate budget.** Every call spent upon an account with no new data was a call denied to one that had some.
- **A stable downstream contract.** Search indexing, reporting, and enrichment all consumed one internal tweet shape, which ingestion had to keep producing regardless of changes in the provider response.
- **Backfill urgency without starvation.** Recovering missing history was usually more urgent than routine refresh, yet it could never be permitted to crowd refresh out for good.
- **A separate search ceiling.** The ingestion-status loop depended upon the search surface, which imposed a limit of its own, independent of the provider's.
- **Bounded, verifiable pagination.** Each download had to cover a known window and continue only while useful history remained, so that under-collection could not pass unnoticed.
- **Recoverability and idle economy.** Thousands of small download tasks had to be replayable, and the ingester had to cost nothing when there was no work to do.

## The Design

Having identified the assets and the threats to them, it is now possible to consider the safeguards. The system is best understood not as a single batch job but as a set of cooperating loops, each with a durable boundary, arranged as a security wheel of detection, avoidance, protection, and recovery. Detection is provided by ingestion-status records, dead-letter queues, and queue-age alarms; avoidance by a cheap validation gate that commits the rate budget only where new data exists; protection by bounded pagination, weighted queue preference, and reset-aware scheduling; and recovery by a durable last-processed position, retried stream writes, and replayable download requests. These safeguards are refined continually rather than fixed once.

The refresh loop begins with a scheduler that reads the oldest eligible accounts from SQL Server and places candidate identifiers onto an SQS queue in small batches. Before any expensive work is scheduled, a validator exercises avoidance directly: it asks whether an account's tweet count or most-recent post has changed since the last check, and whether the account is still accessible, and only then does it raise a download request. That single gate keeps the download workers focused upon accounts with new data instead of cycling through every account on every pass, which is the difference between spending the rate budget and wasting it.

The tweet ingester is an autoscaling ECS task that scales up from zero whenever either queue holds work and back to zero when both drain, so that idle time costs nothing. It turns each download request into a bounded time window: where a request carries no explicit identifiers, synthetic boundary identifiers are generated from timestamps to cover the searchable retention window, and where the oldest and newest bounds are accidentally reversed, the ingester corrects them before calling the provider.

Pagination is the part that was treated most carefully, because the platform's most damaging defect had lived there. After each download the ingester calculates the minimum and maximum identifiers returned; if tweets were found and the minimum is still newer than the requested lower bound, it requeues the same account with the upper bound lowered, and it repeats until the useful range is exhausted. Making that continuation rule explicit and verifiable is what surfaced the fault and restored the intended yield from 6.25% to 100% of each request.

Backfill is given urgency without being allowed to starve routine refresh. Missing history is tracked in its own state, and a scheduled task prepares the outstanding records and places their download requests onto a priority queue. The same ingester consumes both queues, attempting the priority queue roughly three times for each pass at the refresh queue, and falling back at once when the priority queue is empty. One ingestion path therefore serves both purposes, and the balance between them is a matter of configuration rather than a second system to build.

Ingestion is also the boundary at which the provider's response is normalised into the single document shape the rest of the platform consumes. A retrieved response is split into per-tweet documents, only the relevant related records are attached, and each is converted into the internal shape. A quote or a retweet may point at a tweet absent from the response, so the ingester detects the missing references, looks them up, merges them back, and only then converts; a window that cannot be converted safely is skipped rather than allowed to poison the stream. That boundary is what keeps every downstream consumer insulated from changes in the provider response.

The loops are operated as carefully as they are designed. Queues carry dead-letter queues and age alarms; the ingester scales to the depth of its work and to zero beyond it; the scheduler recognises rate-limit responses and waits until the provider's reset time before resuming; Kinesis writes are batched and failed records retried; and the last processed identifier is stored for each account so that the next refresh has a clean starting point. The ingestion-status loop is kept separate because it depends upon the search surface rather than the provider: a scheduler can enqueue hundreds of thousands of identifiers, parallelising the enqueue with TPL Dataflow, while the updater deliberately processes one identifier per invocation and caps its concurrency so that the search ceiling is never breached.

The result is an ingestion platform shaped around its risks. It identifies the assets that must stay fresh and traceable, spends a finite rate budget only where new data exists, bounds and verifies every page of every download, keeps backfill fast without starving refresh, and normalises the provider response at a single boundary so that the rest of the platform, and everything its users depend upon, is never disturbed by how Twitter happens to answer on any given day.
