## Context

The platform is a social-media intelligence service whose core activity depends upon YouTube behaving as a first-class source: recent videos must be searchable alongside every other kind of content, channel profiles must be kept current, and stale or deleted accounts must be removed from the ingestion loop.

The following assets have been identified as central to that activity: the freshness of the ingested data, the finite daily quota granted by the provider API, the schedule that determines which channel is due, the raw feeds, the extracted posts, the account records, the search index, the recovery store, and the correlation evidence by which a single refresh can be traced. We are able to regard these as assets because they are essential to the core activity and the daily operation of the platform; any negative impact upon them would affect not a single request alone but the continuity of the product itself. Were the quota to be wasted, the schedule to drift, or a failure to pass untraced, the data would either grow stale or cease to be trusted.

This reframing is important. It turns ingestion from a single "fetch and save" operation into a question of which assets must be protected, against which threats, and by which safeguards. In the analysis that follows each is considered in turn.

## The Problem

YouTube presents two distinct ingestion problems beneath a single name, and the threats to the assets manifest differently in each. The feeds are the appropriate source for recent videos: inexpensive, straightforward, and available for each channel. The Data API is the appropriate source for account metadata (title, description, thumbnails, reach, and media counts), but it is metered by a hard daily quota. To treat the two as one pipeline would either expend quota upon data that seldom changes or under-refresh the metadata that matters; either outcome places an asset at risk.

The threat that shapes the whole design is waste compounded by silent failure at scale. Quota expended upon unchanged feeds, or upon channels that no longer exist, is a pure loss. With more than 70,000 channels moving as many thousands of small tasks, a single provider throttle, a deleted channel, or a silent feed must not be permitted to halt the loop or to disappear without a trace.

The level of risk follows, as it usually does, from the likelihood of a threat combined with the severity of its impact. Quota carries a high severity, since its exhaustion would degrade the entire product; and silent, untraceable failure carries a higher risk than visible, local incompletion, because the former cannot be recovered with any confidence while the latter can. Our analysis therefore favoured a design that separated the two economics, expended quota deliberately, and kept every failure recoverable, in preference to any single ingenious pipeline.

## Constraints

- **A finite daily quota.** Ten thousand requests each day had to cover the metadata refresh of more than 70,000 channels.
- **Freshness targets.** Every channel had to become eligible for a feed refresh within a 48-hour window.
- **Provider unpredictability.** The provider may throttle, channels may be deleted, and feeds may fall silent; the system had to continue to operate around all three.
- **Recoverability.** With thousands of small tasks in flight, any single channel, message, or provider call had to be replayable without a complete rerun.
- **Traceability.** A distributed, serverless fan-out required a means of following one channel's refresh across every hop.

## The Design

Having identified the assets and the threats to them, it is now possible to consider the safeguards. The design is best understood as a security wheel of detection, avoidance, protection, and recovery, resting upon a single decision: YouTube is treated as two pipelines rather than one. A continuous feed pipeline protects freshness at almost no cost in quota, while a metered metadata pipeline expends the scarce budget deliberately. Detection is provided by explicit feed-state records, dead-letter alarms, dashboards, and a correlation identifier carried across every hop; avoidance by the comparison of a hash before work, the exclusion of inactive channels, and a throttle placed before the provider; protection by quota-sized batching, a durable boundary at every handoff, and retries with backoff; and recovery by replay from object storage, a compressed recovery store, and a search index rebuilt from source. These safeguards are refined continually rather than fixed once.

The feed pipeline begins with a scheduler that selects the channels due for refresh. A channel becomes eligible again only after its 48-hour window has elapsed, and a picker runs each minute, drawing a batch of due channels from SQL Server and passing them to an SQS queue. Reducing the unit of work to a single channel is what allows the remainder of the pipeline to remain small and independently recoverable.

Avoidance is exercised most heavily at the retrieval stage. Before any substantive work is performed, a hash of the incoming feed is compared against the previous one; an unchanged feed is dismissed at negligible cost, and across a 48-hour cycle the majority of feeds are unchanged. The retrieval itself is routed through an API Gateway hop, which affords the team a means of adjusting the rate limit should the provider begin to refuse requests, without redeploying the function behind it. When a feed has changed, its XML is written to S3 together with the correlation identifier and a small serialised handoff record held in the object's metadata, which is sufficient for the following stage to recover its entire context from the object event alone, with nothing retained in process memory.

Parsing distributes the feed in three directions, each across its own durable boundary. Header changes (the account name and description) are placed upon a Kinesis stream, so that profiles may be updated upon their own cadence. Video entries are batched onto an SQS queue, where a further function converts each into a record within a compressed DynamoDB store: the source XML retained compactly, keyed for retrieval by channel, video, and month, and given a ten-year expiry. That store emits a change stream of its own, from which a final stage projects the records into an Elasticsearch index: title, description, links, thumbnails, counts, extracted URLs and hashtags, and normalised index text.

Feed state is made explicit rather than left implicit, and it is the pipeline's principal instrument of detection. A feed that is parsed successfully is moved to a success location and expires quickly; a feed that fails is moved to a failure location and retained for longer, so that it may be examined. It is this single provision that renders the pipeline operable while thousands of small tasks are in motion, for a failure becomes a retained and inspectable object rather than a lost event.

The metadata pipeline operates upon a different cadence and a stricter budget. A daily scheduler reads the active accounts from SQL Server, excludes those channels already known to be inactive, and queues the remainder for refresh. The exclusion of dead channels at the outset is itself a form of avoidance, for quota is never expended in discovering that an account no longer exists.

The following stage groups channels to the batch limit of the provider's channel endpoint, so that a single request covers many accounts and more than 70,000 channels are reduced to approximately 1,450 requests each day against a budget of 10,000. Transient failures are retried with exponential backoff and jitter, whereas permanent conditions are treated as business facts rather than errors: a deleted or missing channel is marked inactive and withdrawn from subsequent runs, and a downstream account update proceeds only where channel data has genuinely been returned. The final stage updates the account record with the current title, description, image, reach, and media counts, and then schedules the affected profile cache to refresh.

Failure is handled identically upon both pipelines. Whatever falls through is delivered to a dead-letter queue whose CloudWatch alarm notifies the team, and the correlation identifier threaded through message attributes and object metadata allows a single channel's passage to be reconstructed from end to end. The result is an ingestion platform shaped around its risks: it separates two data economics, expends the scarce asset deliberately, renders unchanged work almost without cost, and converts every stage into a durable, traceable, and recoverable step rather than an unmonitored call to the provider.
